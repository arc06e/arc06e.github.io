After a century of food consumption of a constant number of daily calories, after 1970 calories consumed have increased by about 20 percent. And the result has been predictableâ€”the conversion of a lean and fit America into the most obese of all developed countries.

A by-product of this increase in food consumption, unfortunately, has been a rise in obesity. Total daily calories of food consumption after 1970 increased by more than 20 percent, enough to add fifty pounds to the average adult each year. Hardly any of this rise resulted from sugar consumption; more than half has come from fats and oils, and most of the rest from flour and cereal. As it stands today, a third of adults and a fifth of children are obese.

Tragically, this increase in obesity has traveled hand in hand with an increase of income inequality and the failure of the United States to conquer poverty. Since 1975, income inequality has continued to increase, producing outcomes such as the day-and-night differences in food consumption in terms of both quantity and quality between those at the top and bottom of income distribution.
